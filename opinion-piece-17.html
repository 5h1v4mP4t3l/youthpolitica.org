The Paradox of Coddling

Meaning well isn’t enough Sumit Paul · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023 A group of students working in a wood shop      If there is anything that characterizes our era, it is an extreme aversion to harm. From childhood we sit through assembly after assembly on the dangers of the internet, strangers, our peers, our family - you name it. Parents hover over their children like ospreys, intent on diving and rescuing them the moment they sniff anything remotely resembling danger. This concerted, exhausted effort to bubble wrap and sanitize the entire world has ironically done more harm than good. It has produced a generation of people with a severe deficiency of character - unprepared to deal with the danger and instability of the real world. Young people reflexively dismiss any suggestion that they, as a collective, are weak. Any attempt to characterize them as so is hand-waved as an inevitable symptom of the accusers’ advanced age, an inevitable part of the cycle of generational resentment. While this claim is not entirely unfair, it is important to recognize that a drop in general resilience can and has been observed. The injustice of the label is not that it is untrue but rather that it formed independent of their control.

     Where lies the origin of our discontent? Child-rearing hasn’t always been like this, not even in living memory. Childhood was once full of adventure and independence that would help people mature into well-adjusted adults. It wasn’t without its problems, though. In the late 20th century, a string of high-profile kidnappings terrorized adults across the United States. Parents, hardwired to protect kids, took to limiting their freedom and movement (funnily enough, a child was far more likely to be kidnapped by a parent than a stranger). The massacre at Columbine at the turn of the millennium prompted the growth of the anti-bullying movement. Anti-bullying is a particularly difficult thing to criticize - which sane person can disagree? And I’m not here to say bullying (in particular, in its more horrid forms) is good or should be encouraged to spur development - rather, the relentless push to end bullying has unnecessarily shielded students from rudimentary forms of teasing that would be conducive to growing up. People, regardless of the time or place will inevitably be met with assaults on their ego and sensibilities - it is wrong to deprive children of the chance to develop resistance. A teacher or principal would tell you that this was never their intent, and they’d be right. The problem is the line between destructive bullying and character-growing teasing is incredibly blurry, and with the spectre of a lawsuit always hanging menacingly in the background, schools erred on the side of safety. Combine this with the aforementioned coddling on the part of parents - the restriction of movement, the constant monitoring, alongside the proliferation of digital technology, children were given endless opportunities to avoid the harshness of life. There’s no risk involved in travelling between a verbally sanitized classroom and your computer desk in your mother’s SUV five times a week. Unvaccinated and unexposed, the child never develops proper risk mitigation or decision-making skills.

     And what becomes of them when they become adolescents and adults? You have a period of time where life is safer and easier than ever which paradoxically has among the highest rates of anxiety, and by corollary, depression and suicide. When the comparative difficulty of grown life hits, young people just are not ready. I don’t want to blame this all on coddling - there are many, many other pitfalls of modern life - but with childhood being as important as it is it’s easy to blame at least part of the problem on stunted emotional growth. Don’t take any of this as a call to blind atavism. I don’t encourage letting kids drink beer or play on railroads. Rather, take it as a call to reassess what we value most in raising children.
