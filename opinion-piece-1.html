A New Technical Course or a Smoke Screen of the Skilled Trades?

Technical courses should remain optional
Fozia Kahloon · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023
A group of students working in a wood shop      The Minister of Education announced on March 10th, that starting from September 2024, students entering high school will require a technical course in order to graduate. The goal of this course is to provide students with more hands-on experiences and technical skills. A greater goal is to direct more students toward the skilled trades.

     In the past economy, higher education meant more financial success. But in today’s economy, we see that because of rising education costs, a dwindling job market and a disproportionate amount of certain academic majors in workforces, pursuing higher education does not guarantee financial success. In fact, statistics show that those in the skilled trades or with an associate degree, earn significantly more than those with a bachelor's degree. Bachelor degree earners are often paid between $34,000 and $97,000. While associate degree earners earn between $27,000 and $68,000. However, this only accounts for the 25th and 75th percentile of workers. This means that 25% of associate degree workers earn more than $68,000. And 25% of bachelor's degree workers earn less than $34,000. Essentially, this just means that recently there's been an influx in occupations requiring not only academic knowledge but technical abilities and work experience. Looking at these statistics, it’s clear that this new course would give a greater advantage to high school students when looking for post-secondary career opportunities.

     However, this course could also be seen as a disregard for the greater benefits bachelor's degree workers get in general. As someone with multiple family members in the skilled trades, while it may be a financially rewarding career, it is emotionally and physically damaging. Most skilled trade workers work extensive hours and work in harsh climates. Let’s compare it to a typical white-collar job. These workers don't have to work overtime, are given paid vacation time and can work in the comfort of their cubicles in the harsh winters. Moreover, skilled trades workers face work instability. A skilled trade worker can’t be sure if they will have more work provided after they’re done with the current one. The board is pushing strongly for more skilled trade opportunities and workers, hence the technical course requirement. Rather than pushing students into a mandatory technical course, while sugarcoating the pathway of a skilled trade worker, the board should leave it as an optional course. Or if the course is going to be mandatory, the government should resolve how to give skilled trade workers a work-life balance without having to worry about work instability.
