Euthanasia: The Gift of Death

Euthanasia should be a choice Maggie Ping · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023 Person comforting a patient (The Conversation)      In June 2016, Quebec passed bill 52 that allowed eligible terminal patients receive medical assistance in dying (MAID), or more legally known as Euthanasia. It is when a patient’s life is ended to prevent further suffering. This would only, however, be an option to adults with capabilities of consent, who are in constant pain (whether psychologically or physically), who have an incurable disease and advancing decline in health. While the debate around this controversial option of health care has always existed, the recent complementary Bill C-39 only added more fuel to the fire.

     Bill C-39 would allow patients with mental disorders (MD-SUMC) to have access to MAID services as of March 17 2024. Until then, policymakers will use the 1 year period as a time of research and observance, to tweak details, consult medical professionals and patients, protect the safety of Canadians, and analyzing the risks: Or in David Lametti, the Justice Minister’s words it’s “to ensure that we move forward on this sensitive and complex issue in a prudent and measured way… [and to] provide time to help provincial and territorial partners and the medical and nursing communities to prepare to deliver MAID in these circumstances.”

     This delay, however, posed a good opportunity for the other side to raise their argument. Throughout the process of legislating Bill C-39, the conservative party prominently disagreed with the expansion. Particularly this year, their voiced concerns suggest that the liberal party (who are pushing Bill C-39) are ignoring experts’ disagreements with the bill, “giving up” on vulnerable Canadians who may benefit more on treatment than suicide. Another big point they make is that mental illness cannot be discerned for irremediability, and without that discernment, how can any doctor be sure that a disorder is treatable or not? Or in the context of MAID, whether a patient is eligible or not?

     The other side, mostly liberals, responded that MAID is simply a service that exercises Canadian’s free will and right to autonomy. And, with the many policies and criteria that remain in place for those concerns, these worries of possibly, regretful suicide should dissipate. One side chooses to be more pro-life, while the other decides to pro-choice.

     Yes, ultimately, giving that choice will manifest the conservatives’ fears, but it also provides those who need it the most. Like the many things in life, there’s a duality; television can be a simple pleasure, or an object of addiction. In the end, it truly depends on what you value the most. Is it the life of a stranger you don’t know, or is it everyone’s ability to choose? Works Referenced McGee, A. (2017, March 2).
