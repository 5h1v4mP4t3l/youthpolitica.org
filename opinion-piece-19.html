The Conservative Party's Failure in Canada

A Reflection of Larger Trends in Canadian Politics Shivam Patel · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023 Scheer gives his concession speech in Regina. (Levinson-King, Robin. “Canada Election: Where It Went Wrong for Conservatives.” BBC News, BBC News, 22 Oct. 2019, www.bbc.com/news/world-us-canada-50103847. Accessed 21 Mar. 2023.)      The Conservative Party of Canada, once a dominant political force in the country, has suffered a major setback in the recent federal election. Despite leading in early polls, the party ultimately failed to secure a majority government, losing seats in key ridings across the country. The reasons behind this failure are complex and multifaceted, but they speak to larger trends in Canadian politics and the challenges faced by the conservative movement in the 21st century.

     One of the key factors contributing to the Conservative Party's defeat was its inability to connect with younger voters. In a country where millennials and Gen Z now outnumber boomers and Gen X, appealing to a younger demographic is essential for any political party looking to succeed in the long term. However, the Conservative Party's messaging and policies on issues like climate change and social justice often seemed out of touch with the concerns of younger Canadians. As a result, the party struggled to make inroads with these voters, who ultimately helped swing key ridings in favor of the Liberals. The Conservative Party's platform on climate change was widely criticized for being inadequate and lacking ambition. Many young Canadians are deeply concerned about the environment and are looking for strong leadership on this issue. The party's failure to articulate a comprehensive plan to tackle climate change likely turned off many younger voters who view it as a pressing crisis that demands urgent action. Similarly, the Conservative Party's stance on social justice issues often seemed out of step with younger Canadians' values. The party was criticized for its opposition to measures like affirmative action and its apparent reluctance to engage with issues like systemic racism and police brutality. Young Canadians, who tend to be more diverse and socially progressive than older generations, were unlikely to support a party that appeared unwilling to address these important issues.

     Another major issue facing the Conservative Party was its lack of diversity and inclusivity. In a country that prides itself on its multiculturalism and progressive values, the Conservative Party often came across as regressive and exclusionary. This was particularly evident in its opposition to measures like the legalization of cannabis and same-sex marriage, which were widely popular among younger and more diverse Canadians. The party's failure to embrace these changing social norms and values likely cost it the support of many potential voters. Its position on these issues was seen by many as out of step with the values of a modern, progressive society. Moreover, the party's messaging and platform did not seem to resonate with Canada's increasingly diverse population. Its reluctance to engage with issues related to immigration and multiculturalism, as well as its perceived hostility to minority rights, made it difficult for the party to win over voters from Canada's growing immigrant communities. In a country where one in five people is foreign-born, this lack of engagement with diversity and multiculturalism was a major handicap for the Conservatives.

      Finally, the Conservative Party's messaging and strategy were often muddled and inconsistent. On issues like health care and the economy, the party seemed unsure of its position and failed to articulate a clear vision for the country's future. This lack of coherence may have turned off voters who were looking for strong leadership and a clear sense of direction. In contrast, the governing Liberal Party was able to articulate a positive and optimistic vision for Canada that resonated with many voters. The Conservative Party's platform focused heavily on economic issues, such as tax cuts and reducing government spending. While these issues are important to many voters, they alone are unlikely to inspire a broad coalition of support. Moreover, the Conservative Party's economic messaging was often overshadowed by controversies and negative campaigning, which may have turned off some voters who were looking for a positive and uplifting message. Additionally, the Conservative Party's messaging on other important issues, such as healthcare and education, was often unclear and lacked substance. While the party did put forward some policy proposals in these areas, they were often overshadowed by its messaging on economic issues and controversies. In contrast, the Liberal Party was able to articulate a clear and compelling vision for Canada that emphasized issues like climate change, social justice, and reconciliation with Indigenous peoples. This vision, which was bolstered by the strong leadership of Prime Minister Justin Trudeau, resonated with many Canadians and helped the party win a second term in office.

      The failure of the Conservative Party in Canada's recent federal election is a complex and multifaceted issue that speaks to larger trends in Canadian politics. While there is no single explanation for the party's defeat, it is clear that a lack of connection with younger voters, a failure to embrace diversity and inclusivity, and a muddled messaging and strategy all played a role. If the Conservative Party hopes to regain its footing and compete in future elections, it will need to address these issues head-on and find new ways to connect with Canadians from all walks of life.
