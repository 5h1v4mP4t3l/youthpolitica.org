Why Ontario School Boards May Be Forced to Cut Down on Education Kayshini Nilendran · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023 Students protesting against cuts to education funding (Getty Images)      We all know that the pandemic committed various crimes in a plethora of areas of our lives. Whether it be changing the way we work or learn, the way we socialize and delve into our hobbies and interests, we all are perfectly aware of the personal sacrifices we had to make to get where we are today. But on that journey, which has now spanned three years and counting, we can agree that certain sacrifices should be avoided especially if there are financial crises and messy politics involved in the matter.

     Education is one of these things. Many, if not all, students had to adapt to new learning models that theoretically (and practically) had no confidence in being functional and successful. Students were forced to give up many of their brightest ambitions, a lot of their dreams were washed down the drain and it took a lot for all of us to realize and overcome those challenges. But even during these times, students came to know who they could trust on getting back on track. The Ontario government, the Ministry of Education - failed to deliver, crashing down on many open promises and leading to many heartbreaks and well-deserved disappointments.

     On March 22, 2023, the Toronto District School Board (TDSB) wrote to Stephen Lecce, Ontario’s Minister of Education, informing the government of the province’s dire financial debt in the name of education. The pandemic had consumed many of the funds that had been reserved for mainstream and specialized programs, and now the TDSB and many similar regional boards were facing an immense crisis. Now that the pandemic is deemed “over” by the government in terms of funding, the predicted 61 million dollar debt (which is still growing by the day) has no concrete way of getting resolved without outside aid.

     As students, we may notice this lack of resources in our classrooms more than we want to admit. Suddenly, many elementary schools aren’t able to afford supplementary teachers, field trips, extracurricular activities and resources in comparison to previous years. Many secondary schools are unable to find the funding to support clubs, unions, societies, and sports teams, unable to find and fill in certain staff positions, trips, competitions, extracurricular activities, and more. Programs such as IB, AP, and honours classes are being cut, as well as support for students with extra learning modifications and IEPs. If we want to be able to support our learning in the best way possible, we need to be aware of and support governmental actions and step-by-step plans that are transparent and promising. We’re here for the students, students that know the value of their position in the classroom and their impact on the society that education is the foundation of. Works Referenced CTV News Toronto.
