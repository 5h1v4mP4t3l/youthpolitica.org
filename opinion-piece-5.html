Defending Ontario’s Restriction of TikTok

The government ought to, sometimes, flex its muscle
Sumit Paul · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023
       In late February, reports emerged that the Ontario government was ruminating about banning TikTok on government-issued devices. Just under two weeks later, the ban came into effect, with Minister of Education Stephen Lecce hinting at a future ban in Ontario schools. The Ford government hardly stands alone - the federal, most provincial and even many municipal governments have instated similar measures. The aforementioned governments have cited “security concerns”, claiming TikTok shares user data, through its Chinese parent company ByteDance, with the Chinese government. TikTok has maintained that it stores its Canadian user data in Singapore and the US and that it is a victim of political tension between the Western world and China.

     While TikTok is administered separately from its Chinese equivalent (Douyin) and ByteDance itself, there is good reason to be skeptical of their claims. Last year, leaked audio tapes revealed that American TikTok employees did not have the prerogative of accessing the data of American users without the approval of engineers in China. This effectively meant, in the words of a member of TikTok’s Trust and Safety team, that “everything is seen in China.” These tapes supported 2021 reports that ByteDance was in “tight control” of the administration of TikTok and therefore had access to American user data. With recent allegations of federal election interference and the background hum of geopolitical friction, it is entirely reasonable for the Canadian governments of all levels to recognize the threat China poses and move to do whatever is in its power to protect the confidential information of its employees. Data is essential to espionage, and the idea of a country giving its enemy an endless supply of information on its citizens is nothing short of foolish. None of this is to speak of the endless potential for distraction and perversity that the app presents to students and government employees alike. TikTok is masterfully designed to be as addicting as possible, with short-form videos and endless scrolling. The very nature of the app promotes narcissism and moral degradation, ailments the state should at least attempt to prevent the development of in its youth. It’s unfortunate, then, that restrictions have only ever been levied against TikTok (and not other apps like Instagram and YouTube), but hypocrisy is a law of nature and government action will only proceed when it is under threat. We should be thankful that the noxious effects of social media are being mitigated, even if not for the right reason.

     Overall, the government has done well maneuvering to protect the data of its citizens and will hopefully take further action within its constitutional limits. Works Referenced Baker-White, E.
