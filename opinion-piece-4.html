Canadian Climate Policy — A Lie?

Canada’s solution to climate change is ineffective at best
Andres Vargas-Daza · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023
Prime Minister Trudeau delivers a speech at the World Climate Change Conference (Reuters)      Canadians know the impact of climate change all too well. Mass forest fires in BC, melting glaciers in the North, sporadic winters in Southern Ontario, and wayward hurricanes in the Maritimes are all consequences of the gradual deterioration in the global climate. In recent years, the federal government has addressed this situation and has aimed to implement policies that will help steer Canada toward a more secure future. However, many of these so-called "adaptation programs", despite the government's firm marketing, are not contributing to the global effort against climate change, and in some cases are even exacerbating the extent of current issues facing Canadians.

     One of the most contentious policies championed by the government as being "greener" is the infamous carbon tax. Endlessly debated and widely misunderstood, the carbon tax is claimed to be the solution to high carbon emissions from businesses. Indeed, the carbon tax does incentivize many businesses to cut carbon emissions they can afford to cut. But for the majority of large, monolithic corporations, the incentive to go greener and the punishment of carbon pricing is a drop in the bucket compared to the massive profits generated by the status quo.

     Of course, many propose a solution of simply increasing the carbon tax to the point where it becomes impossible to ignore. However, in the case of said corporations, the small dent in business simply gets a bit larger, easily being replaced by offloading the additional costs to shareholders and customers. For medium-sized businesses, the impact is massive, and the previously mentioned actions are not enough to prevent their demise at the hands of larger corporations. A more proactive and future-oriented approach must be taken, rather than simply adding an additional tax as a reactionary measure and calling it progress, from which the money is inevitably swallowed up in the mismanagement of government finances anyway.

     Another common "green" position championed by the federal government and by supporting laypersons is the vehement opposition to any sort of investment in fossil fuels for whatever purpose, whether it be exploration, extraction, or exportation. Obviously, such a stance seems wonderful given the ecological and atmospheric damage that fossil fuels cause, and it would be an appropriate one under the right circumstances. As unfortunate as it may be, however, demand for fossil fuels, particularly oil and gas, is only set to increase as the world population grows and developing economies consume more energy. Canada has chosen to reduce future production of its own oil, but at the cost of importing from countries that are either non-democratic regimes, have a poor human rights record, have a poor environmental record, or all of the above. This essentially nullifies any good that ending domestic fossil fuel development might do, and results in no net positive for the global environment.

     Truly, the federal government has pushed ideas that reflect poorly misplaced priorities given the current state of the country and of the world at large.
