Extra Patrols on Transit - A Misguided Solution?

More police is not the answer Sumit Paul · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023        It’s no secret that the TTC has been going through a rough patch. Violence has risen dramatically, increasing by 46% between 2021 and 2022[1]. A poll by the Toronto Star found that about one in six TTC users have begun riding transit less out of concern for their safety[2]. In January of this year, in response to a spate of attacks and even a fatal stabbing, Toronto police announced their plans to deploy 80 additional workers to patrol the transit system to prevent further crime. These deployments ended on March 13, with the enormous cost making them unsustainable beyond the winter. So if hiring extra cops is not a tenable way forward, what is?

     There exists a force in southern California known as the BART (Bay Area Rapid Transit) authority. They are a force of unarmed workers tasked with dealing with non-violent subway crimes, like fare evasion. This unarmed force generally costs less to hire and avoids the violent escalation often associated with the police. The TTC already employs unarmed workers, and this works fine to an extent, but how does one prevent violent crime? Well, for one, existing patrols can be redirected to crime hotspots (places where crime is more likely to take place). But this does not address the root of crime or anti-social behaviour in general. The ideal method of crime prevention prevents the emergence of criminality in the first place. And this can be done by shifting our understanding of the source of crime from purely a deficiency of personal character to the result of the socioeconomic conditions prospective criminals find themselves immersed in. Poverty is a major factor, but it is not the only one. Environments that promote aggression and aversion to law enforcement are a petri dish for criminal behaviour. To remedy this, the city should seek to both improve its relationship with recalcitrant communities and fund programs that work to discourage high-risk individuals from committing crimes. This includes building positive relationships with people in the community to discourage criminal behaviour vicariously (as community members may be more trustworthy of them than police) and running programs that discourage youth from participating in gangs. More broadly, the city should continue to fund mental health resources that mitigate the descent into the psychiatric states that snowball into crime. It should be noted that the erratic nature of the spike in crimes on the TTC does indicate that this is part of a grande mental health crisis.

     As a whole, the city should instate measures that nip crime in the bud to allay the need to spend exorbitant amounts of money on extra patrols. Works Referenced Harvey, L. (2023, February 11).
