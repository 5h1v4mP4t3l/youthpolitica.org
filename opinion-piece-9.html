Critical Race Theory in Canada

The Debate Over Critical Race Theory in Education
Shivam Patel · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023
Parents protested a new anti-racism policy at an Ontario school board saying their children could ‘internalize shame and guilt because they’re white.’ (Unsplash)      A radical academic trend called "Critical Race Theory," which pushes young people to evaluate everyone and everything in life solely on the basis of their race, is something that many of us are acquainted with. Many of us also think it's just something that happens in America. The teachers' unions that have sworn to break these laws, the 27 states that have suggested legislation banning schools from requiring teachers to embrace critical race theory as gospel, and family resistance are all discussed at American school board meetings. We shouldn't presume that because we haven't heard of any such instances in Canada, they don't occur elsewhere. In reality, despite not being a particularly hot or well-known subject, the argument over Critical Race Theory (CRT) and its application to K–12 education has gained traction in Canada, sparking a heated discussion about the role of race and racism in Canadian society.

     This theoretical paradigm—Critical Race Theory (CRT)—looks into the complex interactions between racism and social, economic, and political power systems. CRT has mainly been used in academic settings in Canada to examine the various ways that systematic racism impacts Indigenous peoples and people of color. One of the many theoretical theories that explain how racism is profoundly ingrained in our structures and systems, the theory explores race and racialization. The links between racism, settler colonialism, ableism, sexism, classism, transphobia, and other kinds of discrimination are also studied by CRT. In the field of education, CRT reveals how concepts like justice, equality, color blindness, and impartiality are defined through hegemonic viewpoints and ignore the communal experiences of race and racism that significantly affect the lives of Black, Indigenous, and other racialized students. While some claim that CRT is an essential tool for advancing social equity and variety in Canada, others claim that it is divisive and spreads a harmful and skewed view of the country's past. So how do the points on each side of the dispute appear? Why is CRT instruction in K–12 classrooms seen as being more important to creating a more just and equitable community in Canada? These inquiries demand a nuanced comprehension and an in-depth investigation of the fundamental problems, as well as the implications of this controversial theory on the Canadian educational system and society at large.

     First, those who favor teaching CRT in K–12 institutions contend that doing so is essential for combating systemic prejudice and advancing equality in Canadian society. They contend that CRT offers a crucial paradigm for comprehending how racism functions in Canadian institutions and can aid students in gaining the knowledge and abilities necessary to question and overthrow these structures. Supporters also emphasize that the goal of CRT is to acknowledge and face the fact of racism in Canadian society rather than to incite conflict. CRT can contribute to the development of a more inclusive and compassionate community by introducing students to a variety of viewpoints and experiences.

     However, critics of CRT in Canada contend that it fosters discord and propagates a skewed and detrimental understanding of Canadian history. They contend that CRT is an example of "political correctness," which promotes "cancel culture" and suppresses free expression. Some have even referred to it as a type of "cultural Marxism" and have connected it to rumors about the spread of leftist doctrine in colleges and schools. Additionally, detractors contend that CRT fosters a victimhood mentality that is detrimental to the development of an open and just society.

     Despite the debate, some school boards in Canada have adopted CRT as part of a larger initiative to handle problems of equality and inclusion in K–12 education. For instance, the Peel District School Board in Ontario has created an anti-racism plan that involves including CRT in the course offerings. The Toronto District School Board, in a similar vein, has created an equity action plan that uses CRT as a method for recognizing and combating systemic prejudice. Supporters of CRT have lauded these programs as a crucial move toward establishing more inclusive and equitable classrooms in Canada.

     In conclusion, there are compelling reasons on both ends of the question of whether or not CRT should be taught in K–12 classrooms in Canada. CRT is a crucial instrument for comprehending and combating systemic prejudice in Canadian society, according to some, while others claim it fosters division and a skewed knowledge of the Canadian past. The choice to teach CRT in schools will ultimately rely on carefully weighing these points and a dedication to fostering a more inclusive and just society for all Canadians.
