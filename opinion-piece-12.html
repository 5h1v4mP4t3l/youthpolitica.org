Great Lakes Greater Stakes

Canada’s Historically Largest Freshwater Investment Commitment Kayshini Nilendran · Youth Politica · Posted: May 22 2023 The Great Lakes of Ontario (CNN)      If it’s one thing that brings Canada onto the geopolitical platform, it might as well be our abundant supply of freshwater. Only 2.5% of our planet’s water is freshwater, but even then, almost all of it is present in the ice and earth. Within that statistic, only a smidge more than 1.2% of all freshwater is surface water, of which seven percent is found in Canada. So, it can be said that 20 percent of the world’s freshwater is found in our nation alone. Prevalent in our many lakes, rivers, and other bodies of water, not to mention the Great Lakes which major cities such as Toronto are essentially situated upon.

     Commitments were announced by the national government in the timespan of President Joe Biden’s visit and in the recent budget revision that has financiers fanning themselves with their hats. Environment Minister Steven Guilbeault claims that the new financial indent will bring the Canadian investment to a total of $750 million, $420 million of which is going towards the clean up and restoration efforts of the Great Lakes themselves.

     If we rewind a few years we can note that the Liberals promised to spend, over a decade, greater than a whopping one billion for the restoration of renewable freshwater resources during the 2021 election campaign. The budget that was presented in the last days of March 2023 designated $650 million, in the same time period, for the Fraser River, Mackenzie River, Lake Winnipeg, the Lake of the Woods, Lake Simcoe, the St. Lawrence, and the Great Lakes.

     Now, where is all that money really going? It has been stated that the intention of said funding is to support monitoring, assessment, and restoration work that comes with the prevention of releasing harmful chemicals and reduction of algae blooms. Our freshwater sources have been long subjected to continuous environmental threats in the forms of plastic and toxic chemical pollution, algae blooms that form due to excessive agricultural fertilizer runoff, and invasive species.

     Records show that the half-century-long mission of cleaning up our freshwater sources have been underfunded. The environment has undergone severe, impactful changes over the last fifty years - and yet, we haven’t made much progress, arguably. In comparison to the United States, who we also share the Great Lakes with, we have been “trailing behind” in terms of financial investments and concrete steps of action for a very long time. If the Canadian government, not unlike many of those before them, are unable to uphold the responsibility of national sustainability, there’s only too much we have to lose.
