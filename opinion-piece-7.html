Ontario’s Privatization of Healthcare

The pros and cons Justin Tangpongprush · Youth Politica · Posted: May 23 2023
Premier Ford speaks at a healthcare centre in Toronto      The current Ontario government run by Premier Doug Ford is implementing healthcare policies aimed at providing funding for privatised healthcare in Ontario. This is a shock for many and a bad idea for many more. The idea of having a more privatized healthcare system is unpopular, to say the least; though there is more than one side to this story. The Ford government plans to divert funding away from public health services to spend on private health services such as surgeries done in privately run for-profit clinics.

     This would have always had the consequence of taking away resources from the current public health services in place, but this action has its positives, as the greater number of patients attending a private health clinic relieves public clinics from an overload of patients. “Cataract operations, knee and hip replacements are 'backlogging' Ontario's health system,” says Ford. When these non-urgent surgeries are moved to private clinics, it lets public clinics handle more important surgeries and allows them to handle patients that are needing a public operation covered by Ontario’s universal health insurance. This differentiation of public and private healthcare services would not be privatization, it would simply be a more efficient allocation of resources. Those who need public health insurance and cannot afford to would not be forced into a for-profit clinic, and those who need a for-profit clinic would only have non-urgent operations needed.

     To take the side against the Ford government’s actions, it is not all sunshine and rainbows. Ontario’s Financial Accountability Officer analyzed the Ford government’s healthcare promises and found many issues. Firstly, the Ontario government projects they must spend $87.8 billion a year by 2028, though the report projects they must spend 93.8 billion a year on healthcare, leaving a large 6 billion dollar funding gap every year. Secondly, this plan will only add to the shortage of healthcare workers. The report estimates 23,000 extra nurses and 44,100 new personal support workers will be needed to meet Ford’s plan. This will be a massive problem as Ontario already pays healthcare workers the least out of any province, and it will be a hard sell to anyone in the industry. As well as that, many healthcare unions are directly opposed to the “syphoning” of funding from public hospital care. The drawback of the Ford government’s plan is a big factor to be considered.

     Overall, Doug Ford’s plan is not as bad as its headline might seem, but there are still many issues that need to be addressed before this can become a proper plan.
